## Frequently Asked Questions

### Florida

**What building code does Florida use?**
Florida uses the Florida Building Code (FBC), which is updated on a three-year cycle. The FBC is based on the International Building Code (IBC) and International Residential Code (IRC) with Florida-specific amendments. South Florida's Miami-Dade and Broward Counties fall within the High Velocity Hurricane Zone (HVHZ), which adds wind resistance requirements beyond the standard FBC.

**Do I need a permit for a home addition in Florida?**
Yes. Any addition that increases the square footage or conditioned floor area of a home requires a building permit in Florida. The permit package must include a site plan, floor plans, structural drawings (particularly for wind load compliance), and energy compliance documentation under the Florida Energy Code.

**What are the permit requirements for a pool in Florida?**
Pool permits in Florida require a site plan showing the pool location relative to property lines and the primary structure, structural drawings for the pool shell and deck, electrical drawings for pool equipment, and barrier/fencing documentation to comply with Florida's residential swimming pool safety act.

**How long does permit review take in Florida?**
Permit review timelines vary by county and city. Miami-Dade, Broward, and Palm Beach counties typically take 2–6 weeks for residential projects. Smaller municipalities may review over-the-counter. Projects with complete, code-compliant drawings move significantly faster.

**Does Florida require energy compliance documentation?**
Yes. Florida uses the Florida Energy Code (based on the IECC) and requires energy compliance documentation for new construction and significant additions. The forms and calculation methods are state-specific and differ from California's Title 24 — Florida has its own energy code forms and compliance path.

**Can I convert my garage to living space in Florida?**
Yes, with a permit. A garage conversion in Florida requires a change-of-occupancy permit that addresses habitable space requirements including minimum ceiling height, egress, insulation, ventilation, HVAC, electrical, and energy compliance. Miami-Dade projects must also satisfy HVHZ wind requirements for any new openings.

### Texas

**What building code does Texas use?**
Texas does not have a mandatory statewide residential building code. Cities and counties adopt their own codes — most use the International Residential Code (IRC) or International Building Code (IBC) with local amendments. Austin, Dallas, San Antonio, and most major Texas cities have adopted current model codes. Some rural areas have no adopted building code.

**Do I need a permit in Austin, Texas?**
Yes. The City of Austin requires building permits for new construction, additions, remodels, garage conversions, pools, and most structural alterations. Austin Development Services processes permit applications and conducts plan review. Austin has adopted current versions of the IBC and IRC with local amendments.

**Does Texas require energy compliance documentation?**
Texas cities that have adopted the IRC or IBC are required to comply with the energy code provisions of those documents. Austin and Dallas require energy compliance documentation for new construction. Texas does not have a state-specific energy code — cities that adopt the IRC or IBC are required to comply with the energy code provisions within those model codes.

**What is the permit process in Dallas, Texas?**
Dallas processes permits through the Dallas Development Services department. Residential permits are reviewed for compliance with the Dallas Building Code. Commercial projects go through a more detailed plan review process. Electronic plan submission is available for most project types.

**Can I build an ADU in Texas?**
Yes, in most Texas cities. Austin, Dallas, Houston, and San Antonio all allow accessory dwelling units subject to local zoning rules covering lot size minimums, setbacks, height limits, and parking requirements. Texas does not have statewide ADU reform legislation — each city sets its own ADU rules.

### North Carolina

**What building code does North Carolina use?**
North Carolina uses the North Carolina State Building Code, which is based on the International Building Code (IBC) and International Residential Code (IRC) with state-specific amendments. The NC Building Code is administered locally by county and city building departments. NC updates its code on a regular cycle aligned with ICC model code updates.

**Do I need a permit for a home addition in North Carolina?**
Yes. Any addition that increases the floor area or conditioned space of a home requires a building permit in North Carolina. The permit package must include a site plan, floor plans, elevations, and structural documentation. Energy compliance documentation under the NC Energy Conservation Code is also required for additions involving conditioned space.

**Can I build an ADU or accessory dwelling unit in North Carolina?**
ADU rules in North Carolina are set by local zoning ordinances — there is no statewide ADU reform law in North Carolina. Charlotte, Raleigh, Durham, and Asheville all allow accessory dwelling units subject to local regulations covering lot size, setbacks, height limits, and owner-occupancy requirements. CADTRI researches the specific local rules before preparing permit drawings.

**How long does permit review take in North Carolina?**
Permit review timelines vary by jurisdiction. Charlotte and Raleigh typically process residential permits within 1–3 weeks for straightforward projects. Projects with complete, code-compliant drawings and organized submissions move faster. Commercial projects with full plan review may take longer depending on the building department workload.
