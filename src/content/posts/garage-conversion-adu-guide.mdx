Garage conversions are among the most frequently permitted ADU projects in California. The existing structure, foundation, and roof mean less new construction than a detached ADU, and California state law provides meaningful protections that make garage conversions easier to permit than most property owners expect. Still, the permit process has specific requirements that need to be handled correctly.

## What California Law Says About Garage Conversions

California Government Code Section 65852.2 prohibits cities from requiring replacement parking when an attached or detached garage is converted to an ADU. This is one of the most significant provisions in California ADU law. Before this protection was established, many cities required replacement parking, which often made garage conversions economically impractical.

The law also prohibits cities from requiring setback compliance for ADUs created within existing structures, including garages. This means that a garage that sits three feet from the property line can be converted to an ADU without needing to move or modify the structure to meet the current setback requirement.

These protections apply to both attached garages (those connected to the primary dwelling) and detached garages (separate structures on the property).

## What the Permit Package Requires

### Site Plan

The site plan for a garage conversion documents the location of the existing garage, its distance from property lines, the location of the primary dwelling, and the proposed utility connections for the new unit. Because the replacement parking prohibition applies, the site plan does not need to show replacement parking spaces.

The site plan should clearly label the existing garage as the proposed ADU location and confirm that the conversion does not add new conditioned square footage beyond what is already enclosed.

### Existing and Proposed Floor Plans

The floor plans show the existing garage layout alongside the proposed ADU layout. The conversion typically involves:

- Closing the existing garage door opening and replacing it with an exterior wall with windows and a door
- Adding or relocating interior partitions to create the required habitable rooms
- Adding a kitchen, bathroom, and appropriate sleeping space
- Documenting ceiling height (minimum 7 feet is required for habitable space in most of the garage area)

The floor plans need to show the full scope of interior work, not just a conceptual layout.

### Exterior Elevations

Elevations show the proposed treatment of the former garage door opening, the finish materials for the new wall, and any changes to the exterior appearance of the structure. For projects in design review areas or historic districts, the elevation design receives more scrutiny and should be coordinated with the review authority before the permit set is finalized.

### Structural Documentation

Garage conversions almost always involve structural modifications. Closing a large garage door opening requires a new header beam and modified framing. If the garage slab is being used as the ADU floor, a subfloor system may need to be added for insulation compliance. If walls are being added to the interior, the foundation needs to be able to support them.

For conversions that involve significant structural modifications, a licensed structural engineer needs to prepare and stamp structural drawings and calculations. The architectural drawings need to coordinate with the structural drawings so both can be reviewed together.

### Title 24 Energy Compliance

Garage conversions require a Title 24 energy compliance package. The existing garage was likely an unconditioned space, so the conversion to conditioned habitable space requires compliance with current envelope insulation requirements, lighting standards, and HVAC efficiency requirements.

The Title 24 compliance package is submitted with the permit application and must match the proposed design shown on the architectural drawings.

### CalGreen Checklist

New residential construction and certain alterations in California require a CalGreen compliance checklist. Garage conversions that create a new dwelling unit typically require a CalGreen checklist documenting compliance with the applicable mandatory measures.

## Common Issues That Delay Garage Conversion Permits

**Ceiling height below minimum.** Many garages have slab-to-plate heights between 8 and 9 feet. After adding a subfloor system for insulation, the finished ceiling height may fall below the 7-foot minimum for habitable space in some areas of the unit. This needs to be evaluated during design, not discovered during plan check.

**Missing structural documentation.** Applications submitted without structural drawings for a modified garage door opening are returned as incomplete. The structural scope is not optional.

**Utility connection not addressed.** The ADU needs to document how it will connect to water, sewer, and electrical service. Some properties need to upgrade their service panel or add a new lateral to support the new unit. These need to be addressed in the application, not deferred.

**Title 24 not submitted.** A garage conversion permit application in California without a Title 24 compliance package is incomplete. The building department will not begin plan check until it is provided.

## What the Timeline Looks Like

A well-prepared garage conversion permit application with complete drawings, structural documentation, and Title 24 compliance typically moves through plan check in 4 to 8 weeks in most California jurisdictions, with variations based on the department's current backlog. Projects in cities with over-the-counter ADU review can move faster.

The first submittal is the most important variable in timeline. A complete package reviewed in a single round is almost always faster than an incomplete package that cycles through corrections.
