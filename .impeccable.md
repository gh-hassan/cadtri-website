## Design Context

### Users
Professional B2B clients making a vendor decision under time pressure: general contractors who need permit-ready drawings before pulling a permit, licensed architects with overflow production work, real estate developers managing documentation across pipelines, residential contractors handling ADUs and additions, and property owners navigating the permit process for the first time.

They arrive in evaluation mode. They are comparing CADTRI against alternatives (or against doing it themselves). They need to feel, within the first few seconds, that CADTRI is the most competent option in the room. They read carefully. They notice quality. They do not tolerate anything that looks cheap or auto-generated.

**Job to be done**: Convince a professional that CADTRI will handle their documentation correctly, on time, without surprises — and that engaging them is the lowest-risk path forward.

### Brand Personality
**Three words: Authoritative. Precise. Formidable.**

CADTRI should feel like the most capable firm in the room. Not flashy. Not startup-friendly. Formidable. The site should communicate that every situation a client could face has already been handled, handled correctly, and handled better than anyone else could do it.

**Emotional goal**: Unambiguous confidence. A visitor should leave certain that CADTRI is the expert and that engaging them removes risk from their project.

### Aesthetic Direction
**"Institutional authority meets architectural precision."**

Closest references:
- **SOM (Skidmore, Owings & Merrill)**: Architectural gravitas, no clutter, large type treated as structure
- **McKinsey.com**: Editorial hierarchy, authoritative copy that leads with insight not service lists
- **Foster + Partners**: Precision, whitespace as a statement, not an absence
- **Kirkland & Ellis**: Premium professional services, restrained, zero decorative noise

**Anti-references (avoid at all costs):**
- Cheap vibe-coded / AI-slop: cyan gradients, neon on dark, glassmorphism, glowing borders
- SaaS startup aesthetics: rounded cards everywhere, bouncy animations, feature icon grids
- Generic contractor sites: stock hard hat photos, clip art icons, crowded layouts
- Anything that looks assembled by template rather than designed with intent

**Theme**: Light mode only. Warm cream (#FAF3E1) as dominant ground. Charcoal (#222222) for authority sections and dark bands. Burnt orange (#FF6D1F) used sparingly as the sole accent — CTAs, eyebrow marks, active states. Never decorative.

**Typography system:**
- **Unbounded** (600/700/800) — h1 through h6 display headings. Wide, geometric, commanding. Letter-spacing -0.03em. At large display sizes it should feel like it's carved into the page, not placed on it.
- **Plus Jakarta Sans** (300/400/500/600/700) — everything else: body paragraphs, navigation, UI labels, captions, legal copy. Humanist geometric with slight stroke contrast — creates warmth and tension against Unbounded's pure mechanical geometry. 300 light for body copy, 500/600 for UI labels and emphasis.

### Design Principles
1. **Authority through restraint** — Every element earns its place. No decorative filler. Quality speaks through precision and density of information, not visual noise.
2. **Typography as architecture** — Unbounded headings command the page. Set them large, set them tight, give them air. When in doubt, go bigger and cut the words.
3. **Warm, not cold** — The cream palette prevents sterility. Professional warmth, not corporate chill. Orange is a signal, not a decoration.
4. **Confidence in whitespace** — Premium sites breathe. Generous section spacing signals CADTRI does not rush. Dense layouts feel cheap.
5. **Copy earns the layout** — Headlines read like a senior professional wrote them. No marketing fluff, no feature-list thinking. Every sentence should make a visitor more confident, not just informed.
